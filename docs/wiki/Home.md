# Spine

**Argument-mapping for research outlining** — organise the *skeleton* of a paper's
argument before you bury it in prose and citations.

Spine is a small, local-first desktop app for one specific, painful stage of
research writing: working out the **shape of your argument** — the moves you're
making and how they hold each other up — and keeping that shape legible while you
fill it with evidence.

If you've ever built a beautiful outline, spent a week deep in the literature, and
come back to find you can no longer *see* your own argument under the pile of notes
— Spine is built for exactly that moment.

New here? Start with **[Why Spine?](Why-Spine)** for the idea, then
**[Getting started](Getting-Started)** to build your first map in five minutes.

---

## What it is (in one minute)

- You lay out your argument as **nodes** — typed moves like *premise*, *tension*,
  *conclusion*, *problem framing*, *gap* — joined by **edges** that mean "this
  supports that."
- Each move has a **strength**, and weakness **propagates**: soften one premise and
  every conclusion resting on it visibly softens too. So you can *see* where your
  argument is load-bearing and where it's thin.
- The evidence — quotes, paraphrases, citations — is **attached to a move**, not
  scattered on the canvas. The map stays about the argument; the detail is one click
  away.
- When you're ready to write, a **linear view** lays the whole thing out top to
  bottom, and a **Markdown export** hands a clean, self-describing version to a
  co-author or an LLM.

## What it is *not*

Spine is deliberately **not** a reference manager, **not** a prose word-processor,
and **not** a note vault. Keep your library in Zotero and write your prose in your
editor of choice. Spine owns the one stage those tools handle badly: the high-order
argument.
