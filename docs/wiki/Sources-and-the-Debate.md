# Sources & the debate

Evidence is the **flesh** on the skeleton. In Spine it's attached to a move and read
on demand in the inspector — so the canvas stays about the argument, and the support
is always one click away.

## Supports

Open a move and use **+ Add support**. A support is one use of evidence on one move:

- a **paraphrase, quote, or your own gloss** (free text), and
- optionally a linked **source** — with a source it's a **citation**; without one
  it's your **own reasoning**.

### Reorder by dragging

Grab a support by its grip handle (⠿) and drag it up or down to reorder. The order
is saved.

### Take sides: for / against

Each support has a small stance toggle that cycles **neutral (·) → for (+) →
against (−)**. Use it to record whether a piece of evidence argues *for* or
*against* the claim. This is what lets Spine **document the debate**, not just your
conclusion — at a glance you can see a claim that has three citations for and two
against, which is exactly the kind of tension worth writing about. The stance also
appears in the [Markdown export](Linear-View-and-Export) (`[for]` / `[against]`).

## Citations via BibTeX

Spine is not a reference manager — your library stays in Zotero. But it caches the
*identity* of papers you cite:

- **Import .bib** (toolbar) pulls entries from a BibTeX file. Sources are matched by
  citekey and **refreshed in place** — re-importing never creates duplicates.
- Attach a source to a support to turn it into a citation.

## Provenance: the "monoculture of evidence" check

The **Sources** panel surfaces how your evidence is distributed, so you can catch an
argument that leans too hard on one paper:

- a source's **footprint** — which moves it supports (hover to highlight them);
- a stakes-weighted **load** — how much rests on each source;
- **single points of failure** — a strong conclusion resting on one source. If that
  one paper is wrong, what falls over?

Healthy arguments draw on varied, independent evidence. Spine helps you see when
yours doesn't.
